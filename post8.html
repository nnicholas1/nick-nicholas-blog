Credit Score Requirements: How to Improve Your Score and Meet Lender Expectations

Understanding the Importance of Your Credit Score in the Mortgage Process
Your credit score plays a crucial role in the mortgage application process. It is one of the first things lenders will review when determining your eligibility for a loan. A higher credit score can open the door to better interest rates and more favorable loan terms, while a lower score might limit your options.

In this post, we’ll break down the credit score requirements for different mortgage types, offer practical advice on how to improve your credit score, and provide insight into lender expectations.

What Is a Credit Score and Why Does It Matter?

A credit score is a numerical representation of your creditworthiness, which is based on your credit history and behavior. Lenders use this score to assess the risk of lending you money and to determine whether you’ll be able to repay the loan as agreed.

Credit scores typically range from 300 to 850 and are categorized as follows:

  • Excellent (800–850): You have excellent credit, and lenders will offer you the best interest rates.
  • Good (700–799): Your credit is in great shape, and you’re likely to receive competitive interest rates.
  • Fair (650–699): You may still qualify for a loan, but interest rates may be higher.
  • Poor (550–649): You may struggle to get approved or face higher rates and limited options.
  • Bad (300–549): With a score this low, it can be difficult to secure financing at all.

Credit Score Requirements for Different Mortgage Loans

The minimum credit score requirements for mortgages depend on the loan type. Here’s a breakdown of common mortgage programs and their credit score expectations:

1. Conventional Loans (Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac)

Minimum Credit Score: 620

Ideal Score for Best Rates: 740 and above

Down Payment: Can range from 3% to 20%, depending on your financial profile.

Tip: A higher score (over 740) will not only help you get approved, but you’ll also receive the most competitive interest rates.

2. FHA Loans (Federal Housing Administration)

Minimum Credit Score: 580 (for a 3.5% down payment)

Credit Score Range: FHA loans are more lenient, so you can still qualify with a score as low as 500, but you’ll need a 10% down payment in this case.

Down Payment: 3.5% for scores 580 or higher, 10% for scores below 580.

Tip: FHA loans are a great option if you have less-than-perfect credit. If you’re in the 580–640 range, improving your score by even a few points can significantly impact your interest rate.

3. VA Loans (Veterans Affairs)

Minimum Credit Score: There’s no official minimum, but most lenders prefer a score of at least 620.

Down Payment: 0% (no down payment required).

Tip: Even though there’s no formal minimum credit score, improving your credit will still help you secure better loan terms and a faster approval process.

4. USDA Loans (U.S. Department of Agriculture)

Minimum Credit Score: 640

Down Payment: 0% (zero down payment)

Tip: Like VA loans, USDA loans are a great option for eligible buyers in rural areas, but they come with a higher bar for credit score. If you’re just below the 640 mark, improving your score could make a significant difference.

How to Improve Your Credit Score

Improving your credit score can take time, but small actions today can lead to big results in the future. Here are practical steps you can take to boost your score and position yourself as a more attractive borrower:

1. Check Your Credit Report for Errors

Start by getting a copy of your credit report from the three major credit bureaus: Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. You’re entitled to one free credit report from each bureau every year at AnnualCreditReport.com.

  • Look for mistakes: Check for any incorrect accounts, wrong balances, or fraudulent activity.
  • Dispute errors: If you find errors, dispute them with the credit bureau to have them corrected. This can improve your score quickly if the errors are significant.

2. Pay Your Bills on Time

Your payment history accounts for 35% of your credit score. Late payments, collections, and charge-offs can drastically lower your score.

  • Tip: Set up automatic payments for bills like utilities, credit cards, and loans to ensure you never miss a due date.
  • Tip: If you’ve missed payments in the past, catch up as soon as possible and aim for timely payments moving forward.

3. Reduce Your Credit Card Balances

Your credit utilization (the ratio of your credit card balances to your credit limits) makes up 30% of your credit score.

  • Tip: Aim to use less than 30% of your available credit. For example, if your credit limit is $10,000, try to keep your balance below $3,000.
  • Tip: Paying off high-interest credit cards will not only improve your score but save you money in interest over time.

4. Avoid Opening New Credit Accounts

Every time you apply for credit, a hard inquiry is made on your credit report. These inquiries can cause a small, temporary dip in your score.

  • Tip: Only open new credit accounts when necessary. If you’re planning to apply for a mortgage soon, avoid opening new credit lines.

5. Keep Old Accounts Open

The length of your credit history accounts for 15% of your score. Closing old accounts can reduce your average account age and hurt your score.

  • Tip: Keep your oldest credit cards open, even if you don’t use them regularly. It helps maintain a long credit history.

6. Use a Secured Credit Card

If you have a low score and find it difficult to get approved for traditional credit, a secured credit card may be a great option. You deposit a small amount of money (usually $200–$500), and that becomes your credit limit.

  • Tip: Use your secured card responsibly and pay it off in full each month to build your credit score.

Understanding Lender Expectations

Lenders typically have their own internal requirements for credit scores. However, understanding the general expectations and how they vary by loan type can help you plan.

  • Higher Credit Scores = Lower Interest Rates: The better your credit, the more likely you are to get approved for a mortgage with a competitive interest rate.
  • Lenders Consider More Than Just Your Credit Score: Lenders also review your income, debt-to-income ratio (DTI), employment history, and savings to assess your ability to repay the loan. A good credit score is important, but it’s not the only factor.

How Your Credit Score Affects Mortgage Terms

A higher credit score doesn’t just improve your chances of getting approved—it also impacts the terms of your mortgage. Here’s how:

  • Interest Rates: A higher credit score means a lower interest rate, saving you thousands over the life of your loan.
  • Loan Approval: Some loan programs, like FHA loans, are more lenient with credit scores, but other loans, like conventional loans, require higher scores for approval.
  • Down Payment Requirements: If your credit score is high, you might be able to make a smaller down payment. A score below 700, however, could mean a larger down payment or a higher interest rate.

Conclusion: Take Control of Your Credit Score

Improving your credit score is one of the most powerful steps you can take to make homeownership more accessible. While it takes time, small, consistent efforts can pay off in big ways when you’re ready to apply for a mortgage.
